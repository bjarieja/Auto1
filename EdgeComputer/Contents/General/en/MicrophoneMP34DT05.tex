\chapter{Microphone MP34DT05-A}\label{MicrophoneMP34DT05}

\section{Background}

\bigskip
Microphones are devices designed to convert acoustic energy (sound waves) into electrical signals. They are used in numerous applications, from studio recording and live performances to mobile communication and embedded sensing systems \cite{Eargle:2012}. Key categories of microphones include:

\subsection{Dynamic Microphones}

\bigskip
These rely on electromagnetic induction: a diaphragm attached to a coil moves within a magnetic field to produce a voltage. Dynamic microphones are rugged and handle high sound pressure levels effectively.

\subsection{Condenser Microphones}

\bigskip
These operate using a charged diaphragm and backplate, creating a capacitor whose capacitance varies with sound-induced vibrations. Known for accurate frequency response and sensitivity, condenser microphones are widely used in studio recording and precise measurement applications \cite{Ballou:2015}.

\subsection{MEMS (Micro-Electro-Mechanical Systems) Microphones}

\bigskip
These miniature microphones are fabricated using silicon-based micro-machining processes. MEMS microphones offer small form factor, low power consumption, and an easily integrated digital or analog output. They are commonly found in smartphones, wearables, and Internet of Things (IoT) devices \cite{STMicroelectronics:2021b}.

\bigskip
Regardless of type, microphones must be carefully selected and positioned to achieve the desired balance of frequency response, sensitivity, and noise performance. Enclosures, ambient temperature, humidity, and electromagnetic interference can also affect microphone performance.

\section{Considerations}

\bigskip
When integrating microphones into embedded systems or electronic devices, the following considerations often apply:

\subsection{Signal Conditioning}

\bigskip
Many modern MEMS microphones provide built-in amplification or digitization (e.g., PDM output), simplifying downstream signal processing. Still, designers must ensure correct voltage levels and clock signals are provided.

\subsection{Mounting and Orientation}

\bigskip
Proper mounting ensures minimal mechanical stress and optimal exposure to the acoustic environment. Misalignment or obstruction can degrade frequency response and introduce unwanted resonances.

\subsection{Environmental Factors}

\bigskip
Temperature changes, humidity, and external vibrations can influence microphone sensitivity or introduce noise. Special materials, enclosures, or protective mesh are sometimes required to mitigate environmental effects \cite{Eargle:2012}.

\subsection{Power and Grounding}

\bigskip
Clean and stable power supplies, as well as careful grounding and shielding, are critical for noise-sensitive components like microphones. Ground loops or power supply noise can degrade audio quality significantly.

\section{Calibration and Testing of the Microphone}

\bigskip
Calibration and testing ensure the microphone’s output corresponds accurately to known sound pressure levels (SPL). A typical procedure involves:

\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{Reference Tone:} Expose the microphone to a calibrated sound source, such as a 94 dB SPL tone at 1 kHz.
	
	\item \textbf{Signal Capture and Analysis:} Record the microphone output and convert it to a measurable format (e.g., PCM) using appropriate hardware or software tools. Compare the digital amplitude against the known reference level.
	
	\item \textbf{Gain Adjustment:} Apply any necessary digital or analog gain to align the measured output with the reference SPL. This step ensures consistent loudness across different devices.
	
	\item \textbf{Frequency Response Verification:} Sweep across the microphone’s intended bandwidth (e.g., 100 Hz to 10 kHz for speech-focused applications) to check amplitude consistency and detect irregularities.
	
	\item \textbf{Noise Floor and Distortion Tests:} Measure the microphone’s noise floor under quiet conditions. Record a pure tone at varying amplitudes to calculate total harmonic distortion (THD), helping to identify non-linearities.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Algorithms for Evaluating a Microphone}

\bigskip
Several analytical methods can be employed to assess microphone performance in both research and practical deployments:

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR):} Compares the strength of the desired signal to the background noise level. A higher SNR indicates clearer audio.
	
	\item \textbf{Total Harmonic Distortion (THD):} Evaluates how much the microphone alters a pure test tone. Lower THD values reflect more faithful signal reproduction \cite{Ward:2001}.
	
	\item \textbf{Spectral Analysis (FFT/Spectrogram):} Displays frequency components over time, useful for detecting resonance, filter roll-offs, or interference at specific frequencies.
	
	\item \textbf{Impulse Response:} Examines how quickly and accurately the microphone responds to short transient sounds, revealing phase or time-domain artifacts.
	
	\item \textbf{Cross-correlation:} Compares a reference audio signal to the microphone’s output, providing insights into timing shifts or fidelity loss.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Sound and Frequencies}\label{Sound and Frequencies}

Sound, also known as acoustic pressure, more precisely refers to a single sound frequency that causes mechanical deformation in the medium of air, which propagates as a longitudinal wave.

The frequency $f$, measured in hertz (Hz), represents the number of pressure fluctuations in the air per second within a sound wave. For example, the human auditory system can typically perceive frequencies in the range of approximately 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz.

The faster the oscillations occur, the higher the frequency and thus the perceived pitch of the sound. The larger the amplitude, the louder the sound.

Loudness is characterized by the amplitude, which is generated by the sound pressure. Loudness is measured in decibels (dB). It is defined relative to a reference sound pressure level. The human hearing threshold is defined at \( P_{\text{ref}} = 2 \cdot 10^{-5}\,\text{Pa} \). Any sound below this level (i.e., with negative dB values) is generally inaudible to the human ear~\cite{Luders:2017}.

In sound processing systems, these oscillations are typically captured mechanically, most commonly using microphones.

\subsection{Difference Between Tone, Timbre, and Noise}

A tone can be described by a harmonic function with a constant frequency. \cite{Rossing:2002} 
In contrast, a timbre (or sound in a musical context) is a more complex but still periodic acoustic signal.  
A key characteristic of a timbre is the superposition of a fundamental frequency with its overtones (harmonics). \cite{Zwicker:1999}
Noise, on the other hand, is generally a non-periodic signal and exhibits irregular and varying structures. \cite{Smith:2007}

\subsection{Microphones}

The fundamental operating principles of different types of microphones are largely similar.  
Sound waves set a diaphragm into motion, and this mechanical vibration is converted into electrical signals using various transduction methods (see Figure~\ref{fig:Mikrofonarten}).

Since computer processors cannot directly process these analog electrical signals, they must be converted into digital signals by electronic converters.  
First, we consider several methods suitable for transforming diaphragm vibrations into electrical signals. These include physical principles such as electromagnetic induction, changes in capacitance, piezoelectric effects, or variations in electrical resistance (as in carbon microphones).

In addition to these commonly used sound transduction methods, there are also a number of other, less widespread techniques.

\subsubsection{Condenser Microphone}

In a condenser microphone (see Figure~\ref{fig:Kondensator Mikrofon}), the diaphragm forms one of the electrodes of a capacitor.  
When sound waves cause the diaphragm to move, the distance to the back electrode changes, resulting in a variation in the capacitance of the capacitor.  
The capacitor is charged via a high-impedance resistor. Changes in capacitance lead to corresponding changes in the voltage across the capacitor.  
This voltage variation can be measured using a suitable voltage detection circuit.

Subsequently, the analog electrical signal must be converted into a digital signal (see Figure~\ref{fig:AD-Wandler}).  
This step is necessary because computer processors can only process digital signals.  
To achieve this, analog-to-digital converters (ADCs) are used.  
These ADCs can either be integrated directly into the microphone or be external components.  
Modern computers typically have built-in ADCs; however, in professional audio applications, high-quality external ADCs are often preferred.

In this project, a microphone with an integrated ADC is used.  
The resulting digital signal is transmitted to the computer in binary form via a USB interface.

\subsection{WAV File Format}

Uncompressed audio data is typically stored in the WAV file format (see Figure~\ref{fig:AufbauWave}).  
This file format was introduced by Microsoft and IBM in 1991 for the digital storage of audio data.  
It is based on the RIFF (Resource Interchange File Format) structure.

A key feature of the RIFF format is that it stores metadata about the structure of the file content at the beginning of the file.  
WAV audio files are essentially RIFF files with a WAVE container.  
These usually contain raw audio data encoded using PCM (Pulse-Code Modulation), a method that converts analog signals into digital form through pulse modulation.

Typically, a RIFF audio file with a WAVE container includes two subchunks:
\begin{itemize}
	\item The \texttt{fmt} chunk, which defines the format of the audio data and how it is stored.
	\item The \texttt{data} chunk, which contains the actual audio samples encoded in the format specified in the \texttt{fmt} chunk.
\end{itemize}

The first 4 bytes of a WAV file contain the ASCII characters \texttt{RIFF}.  
The next 4 bytes store the size of the remaining data in the file (in little-endian format).  
Finally, another 4 bytes indicate the file type—\texttt{WAVE} in this case.

\label{ascii}
ASCII stands for \glqq American Standard Code for Information Interchange\grqq~and is a 7-bit character encoding standard corresponding to ISO 646.

The encoding defines a total of 128 characters, of which 95 are printable and 33 are non-printable control characters.  
Some examples include:
\begin{itemize}
	\item Letters: A, B, C, …, Z, a, b, c, …, z
	\item Digits: 0, 1, 2, …, 9
	\item Punctuation marks: !, ", \#, \$, \%, \&
\end{itemize}

A detailed representation is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ASCII}.

It is important to note that each character corresponds to a defined numerical value.

\begin{figure}[ht]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{Microphon/ASCII.png}
	\caption{Symbols and numerical values of the ASCII format}
	\label{fig:ASCII}
\end{figure}

The terms little-endian (LE) and big-endian (BE) indicate the byte order in which numerical values are stored and interpreted.

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Big-endian (BE)} stores the most significant byte first. When reading multiple bytes, the first byte (or the lowest memory address) holds the highest value.
	\item \textbf{Little-endian (LE)} stores the least significant byte first. When reading multiple bytes, the first byte contains the smallest value.
\end{itemize}

In the \glqq fmt\grqq~(format) subchunk, all specifications regarding the structure of the following audio data are defined:

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Format Identifier:} The first 4 bytes mark the start of the format subchunk with the ASCII characters \texttt{f}, \texttt{m}, \texttt{t}, and a space (BE).
	\item \textbf{Subchunk1Size:} The next 4 bytes specify the remaining size of the \texttt{fmt} subchunk (LE).
	\item \textbf{AudioFormat:} The following 2 bytes indicate the audio encoding format (typically 1 = PCM, uncompressed) (LE).
	\item \textbf{NumChannels:} These 2 bytes define the number of audio channels (mono = 1, stereo = 2, etc.) (LE).
	\item \textbf{SampleRate:} The next 4 bytes indicate the sampling rate of the audio signal in hertz (Hz) (LE).
	\item \textbf{ByteRate:} These 4 bytes represent the byte rate, i.e., the number of bytes transmitted per second (LE).
	\item \textbf{BlockAlign:} These 2 bytes specify the block alignment. A block is defined as the number of bytes per sample multiplied by the number of channels.  
	\[
	\text{BlockAlign} = \left(\frac{\text{BitsPerSample} \times \text{NumChannels}}{8}\right)
	\]
	\item \textbf{BitsPerSample:} The final 2 bytes in this example specify the bit depth per audio sample. 8 bits correspond to one byte (LE).
\end{itemize}


If a format other than PCM were used for the audio data, an additional subchunk would follow the current section to specify that particular format.  
\bigskip

Finally, the actual audio samples are stored in the \texttt{data} subchunk, as specified above.  
This subchunk begins with the ASCII characters \texttt{data} in the first 4 bytes.  
The following 4 bytes (in little-endian format) indicate the size of the audio data in bytes.

\subsection{Smoothing Audio Files}

Exponential smoothing is used for smoothing the audio file.  
It is one of the fundamental algorithms for smoothing time series data.  
In exponential smoothing, the importance of previous values is weighted by the parameter \( \alpha \).  
The smaller \( \alpha \), the more weight is given to previous data points.  
If the audio file contains a high level of noise, a lower \( \alpha \) should be chosen;  
if the goal is to minimally alter the original audio, a larger \( \alpha \) is preferable.  
The usable range for \( \alpha \) is \( 1 \geq \alpha \geq 0 \).

The formula used for exponential smoothing is:

\begin{itemize}
	\item[] \( S_{i} = \alpha \cdot data_i + (1 - \alpha) \cdot S_{i-1} \)
	\item[] \( S_{i} \): Smoothed value at time point \( i \)
	\item[] \( \alpha \): Smoothing factor
	\item[] \( data_i \): Original data value at time point \( i \)
	\item[] \( S_{i-1} \): Smoothed value at time point \( i - 1 \)
\end{itemize}

The required modules for implementation are \PYTHON{wave} and \PYTHON{numpy}.  
To use the program, \FILE{inputAudio.wav} must be replaced with the path to the WAV file that is to be smoothed.

\subsection{Pitch and Speed Modification}

When changing pitch or applying frequency scaling, the digital signal is adjusted afterward without affecting other aspects of the sound, such as playback speed.  
The phenomenon in which altering the frequency also changes the length (and thus the duration) of the audio file is avoided by using a method called \textit{pitch shifting}.

A similar situation occurs when changing playback speed.  
Simply altering the playback rate results in pitch distortion.  
Therefore, the goal is to compress or stretch the playback duration without affecting the spectral content—this process is known as \textit{time stretching}.

Thus, for both operations, the following applies:  
In order to change one parameter (pitch or time), the interdependence between frequency and time scaling must be suppressed.  
This is achieved using \PYTHON{librosa.effects.pitch\_shift} and \PYTHON{librosa.effects.time\_stretch}.

The method is based on a phase vocoder that applies the \textit{Fast Fourier Transform} (FFT) (see Chapter~\ref{Frequenzanalyse}).  
Below, the algorithm behind the phase vocoder is briefly and simplified for explanation purposes.

The first step in phase vocoder analysis is the windowing function.  
In this step, the digital sample signal is divided into multiple time segments, and each segment is multiplied by an envelope function.  
The shape of the window function influences the resulting sound of the processed audio.  
Common window shapes for phase vocoders are usually bell-shaped.  
The effect of this signal modification is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:Window-Funktion}.


\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{Microphon/PhaseVocoder.jpg}
    \caption{Window Function}
    \label{fig:Window-Funktion}
\end{figure}

Subsequently, each windowed segment undergoes spectral analysis using the Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT).  
The STFT algorithm applies the Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) successively to the windowed sample segments.  
Therefore, the STFT can be understood as a sequence of DFTs.  
A DFT analysis is discrete in both time and frequency domains, resulting in the representation of isolated spectral lines.

Next, the number of frequency bands must be defined.  
This is achieved by mirroring each positive frequency band into the negative (imaginary) domain.  
Both mirrored frequency bands represent halves of the amplitude of the measured real frequency band.  
Among the various post-processing steps, the phase vocoder discards the negative frequencies and doubles the amplitude of the positive ones.

The result of a single FFT on a windowed sample is referred to as a \textit{frame}.  
A frame consists of two sets of values: the magnitudes (or amplitudes) of all frequency bands, and the initial phases of those bands.  
Additionally, the phase difference relative to the previous frame is computed, providing insight into where the pitch may lie within a given frequency band.  
The combination of amplitude and phase difference information for each band is referred to as a \textit{bin}.  
These amplitude-phase data pairs are represented as x- and y-coordinates for each bin.

Another key analysis parameter is the \textit{hop size}, which defines the distance between the starting points of consecutive windows.  
Closely related is the overlap factor, which influences the amount of information captured in the analysis.  
An excessively high overlap factor can lead to a blurred or smeared audio result.

Based on the collected analysis data, resynthesis of the signal is performed.  
This involves applying an inverse STFT, during which the ongoing tracking of phase differences from frame to frame is executed—a process referred to as \textit{phase unwrapping}.

The resynthesis phase is where the procedures for pitch shifting and time stretching diverge:

\begin{itemize}
	\item For \textbf{pitch shifting}, the frames are played back at the same rate as in the original analysis.  
	However, the frequencies of the entire spectrum can be proportionally shifted upward or downward.  
	This results in a change in pitch without altering the playback speed.
	
	\item For \textbf{time stretching}, the rate at which the frames are reconstructed is either increased or decreased, depending on the desired playback speed.  
	The phase vocoder interpolates between individual frames, adding new data points to create smoother transitions.  
	Since the spectral content of each frame remains unchanged, modifying the speed does not affect the pitch.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Frequency Analysis} \label{Frequenzanalyse}

Sound can be recorded using a microphone, which captures the voltage–time profile of the signal.  
However, it is difficult to identify the signal components directly in this form.  
Through frequency analysis, the signal characteristics can be determined, and important patterns can be recognized.

One method for frequency analysis is spectral analysis based on the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT).  
FFT is a widely used technique in audio and acoustic measurement technology.  
It decomposes a discrete-time signal into its frequency components, allowing for detailed analysis.  
Spectral analysis is a fundamental tool in signal processing.  
It is used to investigate the frequency content of a signal, identifying which frequency components are present and how they are distributed~\Mynote{cite}.

FFT is an algorithm for calculating the Fourier series, applied to discrete signals as the Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT).

The Fourier series representation for the discrete Fourier transform is given in Equation~\ref{eq: DFT1}, with the discrete Fourier coefficients defined in Equation~\ref{eq: DFT2}.

\begin{equation}
	f_T(t) = \sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty} \gamma_k e^{ik\omega t} \quad \text{with} \quad \omega = \frac{2\pi}{\tau}
\end{equation}
\label{eq: DFT1}

\begin{equation}
	\gamma_k \equiv \gamma_k(f) = \frac{1}{T} \cdot \int_{-T/2}^{T/2} f_T(\tau) e^{-ik\omega \tau} \, d\tau 
	\quad \text{for} \quad k = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \dots
\end{equation}
\label{eq: DFT2}

\begin{itemize}
	\item[] Where:
	\item[] \( f_T \): Time-continuous, \( T \)-periodic signal
	\item[] \( e^{-ik\omega \tau} \): Fundamental frequency component
	\item[] \( \omega = \frac{2\pi}{\tau} \): Frequency
	\item[] \( \gamma_k \): Amplitude factor of the \( k \)-th harmonic
\end{itemize}

The Fourier series formula generally describes the representation of periodic signals in the frequency domain as a sum of sine and cosine waves of different frequencies.  
In the case of the Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT), continuous, periodic signals are converted into discrete, non-periodic signals~\Mynote{cite}.

To perform such a computational operation, the following spectral analysis parameters must be defined:

\begin{itemize}
	\item Number of sampled points (NFFT)
	\item Sampling frequency
\end{itemize}

These parameters determine the frequency resolution of the analysis.

\subsection{Decibels}

In electronics one often wants to represent the ratio of two signals on a logarithmic scale. For this we use the decibel, $dB$. If we have two powers $P_1$ and $P_2$

$$dB = 10 \log_{10} \left(\frac{P_2}{P_1}\right)$$

or equivalently -- when comparing two signals with the same kind of waveform --

$$dB = 20 \log_{10} \left(\frac{V_2}{VP_1}\right).$$


Note $dB$ is a relative unit of measurement, i.e. \textit{This amplifier has a gain of 10 dB.} dB can be positive or 
negative. For example, $+10 dB$ corresponds to $P_2$ greater than $P_1$ by a factor of $10$, and $-3 dB$ 
corresponds to $P_2$ less than $P_1$ by approximately a factor of $2$. 

For an absolute measure on a logarithmic scale there are a variety of other units. A common one is $dBm$.

$$dBm = 10 \log_{10} (P [mW])$$

Zero $dBm$ corresponds to $1 mW$ of power. And in a $50 \Omega$ system $0 dBm$ corresponds to $220 mVrms$.

\subsection{Puls-Dichtemodulation}

Pulsdichtemodulation (PDM) ist eine faszinierende Technik, die im Arduino Nano 33 BLE Sense verwendet wird, um Audiosignale zu digitalisieren. PDM wandelt analoge Audiosignale in digitale Daten um, indem es die Dichte der Impulse misst.
Statt kontinuierlicher Werte verwendet PDM nur zwei Zustände: 1 (Impuls vorhanden) oder 0 (kein Impuls).  Um das Mikrofon zu verwenden, steht die Bibliothek \FILE{pdm.h} zur Verfügung. PDM arbeitet mit einer hohen Samplingrate, um genaue Audioinformationen zu erfassen. Die Impulse werden als Bitstream übertragen, der später in Audiodaten umgewandelt wird. 

Die Puls-Dichtemodulation (PDM) ist eine interessante Technik, die sowohl Vor- als auch Nachteile aufweist. 

Die Puls-Dichtemodulation (PDM) hat folgende Vorteile:


\begin{itemize}
    \item Einfachheit: PDM ist weniger komplex als einige andere Modulationsverfahren. Die Implementierung von Sendern und Empfängern für PDM ist vergleichsweise unkompliziert.
    \item Robustheit gegenüber Störungen: PDM ist widerstandsfähig gegenüber Rauschen und Interferenzen.
    \item Hohe Samplingrate: PDM arbeitet mit einer hohen Abtastrate, um genaue Audioinformationen zu erfassen.
    \item Geringe Verluste: Im Vergleich zu analogen Steuerungen verursacht PDM weniger Verluste, da es die Versorgungsspannung schnell ein- und ausschaltet.
\end{itemize}

\bigskip

Die Nachteile der Puls-Dichtemodulation (PDM) sind:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Große Lasten: Die Steuerung großer Verbraucher erfordert hohe Spannungen und Ströme. Standard-Analogschaltungen und DACs sind hierbei weniger effizient.
    \item Wärmeentwicklung: Leistungstransistoren, die in PDM-Schaltungen verwendet werden, erzeugen Wärme und Verluste.
\end{itemize}

Insgesamt ist PDM eine leistungsstarke Technik, die in verschiedenen Anwendungen wie Spracherkennung, Klanganalyse und Datenübertragung eingesetzt wird. Es ist wichtig, die Vor- und Nachteile je nach Kontext zu berücksichtigen

\PYTHON{MaxSampleVoltage} wird berechnet, indem der maximale Samplewert \PYTHON{maxSampleValue} mit der Referenzspannung des Mikrocontrollers („Vref = 3,3 V“) multipliziert und durch den maximalen Wert eines 16-Bit-Signed-Integers (32767) dividiert wird. 32767 ist der maximale Wert eines 16-Bit-Signed-Integers, der der maximalen Amplitude des Audiosignals entspricht.
Der dB SPL-Wert („dBspl“) wird mit der Formel „20 * log10(Voltage / Vrms) + sensitivity“ berechnet [Quelle der Fomel: PDF Decibels Formula https://physicscourses.colorado.edu/phys3330/phys3330\_sp19/resources/Decibels\_Phys\_3330.pdf University of Colorado System], wobei „Voltage“ der berechnete „maxSampleVoltage“ ist, und „sensitivity“ die Empfindlichkeit des Mikrofons in dBV (Dezibel-Volt) ist. „Vrms“ ist die RMS-Spannung (Root Mean Square), die einem Schalldruckpegel von 94 dB SPL entspricht. Dieser Wert wurde experimentell ermittelt.
